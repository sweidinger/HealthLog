# R-2 — Detail-page patterns across the leading medication trackers

External research informing the v1.5.5 detail-page rebuild. Walks six reference apps, then synthesises an architecture verdict for `/medications/[id]/page.tsx`. Citations point at official Apple documentation, App Store listings, design-press, German-language user guides, and patient-forum threads where the user-side complaint carries the signal.

## 1. Apple Health — Medications, individual detail page (iOS 17, iOS 18+)

The reference. Apple's official guide pins the scroll order: drug name + dose at the top (no status badge — active is the absence of archived), a **Log** strip with day-of-week circles, then **Details** (icon, nickname, notes) and at the bottom **Options** where destructive controls live ([Apple Support, "Add and log medications"](https://support.apple.com/en-us/105064); [Apple Support guide](https://support.apple.com/guide/iphone/track-your-medications-iph811670c81/ios)). The schedule editor opens behind a **Reset Schedule** label rather than inline — Apple gates the destructive shape of a schedule rewrite explicitly. iOS 18 added **On a Cyclical Schedule** and **On Specific Days of the Week**, surfaced as dedicated rows inside the schedule editor — explicit acknowledgement that flexible-rhythm cases (HRT, GLP-1, cycled steroids) deserve top-level layout.

Intake history is a frequency rollup drillable to day / week / monthly / half-year / year ([TidBITS overview](https://tidbits.com/2022/10/07/an-apple-a-day-ios-16-medications-feature-provides-alerts-logging-and-peace-of-mind/)). Per-event editing is buried; patients on Apple Discussions complain "with iOS 17 the delete function has disappeared" and "the official instructions do not match what I see on my phone" ([discussions.apple.com/thread/254888869](https://discussions.apple.com/thread/254888869)). The split between **Archive** (swipe-left, preserves history, no future reminders) and **Delete Medication** (purges everything, bottom of Options) is the cleanest in the category — but discoverability is so poor that "where is delete?" dominates the forum signal. Notifications are a global Medications-section toggle, not per-drug; there is no per-medication pause; titration is implicit through the cyclical-schedule editor. Visual rhythm is pure settings-app vernacular: grouped-inset cards, subtle separators, no per-drug accent (the user-chosen icon carries identity), 17 pt body / 22 pt headline / SF Pro Rounded for section labels.

## 2. MyTherapy — German-origin, condition-module, patient-focused

MyTherapy's detail page is a single scrolling form: drug name + dose, then **Einnahmezeiten** as a stack of time rows (each with per-time settings), per-weekday adjustments, **Erinnerungston**, **Vorratsverwaltung** ([meine-gesundheitshelfer.online walk-through](https://www.meine-gesundheitshelfer.online/mytherapy-medikamente-eingeben/)). Save is **Sichern**, top-right. Intake history isn't on the detail page at all — it lives in a sibling **Tagebuch → Meine Werte → Medikamente** screen, status glyphs ("grauer Haken" = taken, "rotes Kreuz" = skipped/pending), tap a day to open an edit sheet ([Nacherfassen walk-through](https://www.meine-gesundheitshelfer.online/medikamente-nacherfassen-in-mytherapy/)). A parallel **Logbuch** opens from a clock glyph on **Heute** with **erledigt**, **übersprungen**, **automatisch übersprungen**. The split — settings on the detail page, history in a separate diary surface — is the source of the dominant user-voice thread; a representative request describes "medications such as blood pressure, blood thinners, anticonvulsants (and others) that are periodically held back" and asks for a pause primitive the current detail surface doesn't expose ([mytherapy.uservoice.com #32753707](https://mytherapy.uservoice.com/forums/304831-collection-of-ideas-for-mytherapy/suggestions/32753707-pause-all-medications)). Notifications are per-time, not per-medication; pause exists only as "skip today" inside a reminder notification; titration is not modelled. Visual rhythm is dense form-row vernacular, white background, no card grouping — closer to a settings list than a hero surface.

## 3. Medisafe — US market leader, dense surface

Medisafe's detail page is reached from the bottom-bar **Medications** tab; tapping the row opens a read view, **a pencil icon top-right** enters edit mode ([Tech-Enhanced Life walk-through](https://www.techenhancedlife.com/citizen-research/how-use-medisafe-app)). The surface stacks pill image + name + dose, then frequency, refill thresholds, **Instructions** notes, and a doctor-described "what does this medication do" panel. Intake history is not the headline — the daily pillbox metaphor (morning / afternoon / evening / night) carries the history affordance from the home screen; the detail page surfaces only the most recent intakes. Delete lives at the bottom of edit mode ("look for the 'delete' choice at the bottom of the screen"). Pause is absent as a labelled control — users either skip from the reminder or delete and re-add. Notifications are per-medication. Visual rhythm is pillbox-iconography heavy, four-quadrant colour wheel, drug-photo thumbnails — bright, friendly, comparatively cluttered next to Apple Health.

## 4. Round Health — design-press darling, restrained typography

Round Health's signature primitive is the **reminder window** — a range of time around a target dose, with notifications at the beginning, middle, and end of the window ([iMedicalApps review](https://www.imedicalapps.com/2016/11/round-health-app-personal-assistant-pill-taking/)). The detail page stacks drug name + nickname, the window as a visual band, frequency type (**scheduled** / **as-needed**), stock count, notes. Edit and delete are mode-switched rather than always-visible. Intake is logged from the notification (swipe left) or from a circle on the detail card (tap to fill). Round trades feature breadth for restraint — users praise the "beautiful and satisfying" interface and the "subtle, persistent reminders [that] go beyond awkward phone alarms" ([SteadyHealth](https://www.steadyhealth.com/review/round-health-app-a-simple-medication-reminder-and-tracker-for-iphone-and-apple-watch); [AppGrooves reviews](https://appgrooves.com/ios/1059591124/round-health/circadian-design-inc)). Visual rhythm: large drug-name typography, generous white space, single muted accent per medication (colour swatch picked on creation), no chrome-heavy cards. Titration absent; pause absent.

## 5. Dosecast — rolling-cadence-native

Dosecast's detail page is built around schedule flexibility: the top exposes **daily/weekly/monthly**, **every X days/weeks**, and the distinguishing **"X hours/days since last dose"** rolling-cadence mode ([Dosecast App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/dosecast-my-pill-reminder-app/id365191644); [Montuno features](https://www.montunosoftware.com/features/)). A reviewer captures the why: "the only medication reminder that automatically adjusts your next reminder based on the amount of hours intervals … every 4 hours after last dosage taken." Below the schedule the detail page lists **drug name, type, dosage, strength, route**, then **doctor + pharmacy + prescription number** as linkable rows, then **maximum doses per day** as an explicit overdose-guard, then expiration, then photo. History lives in a separate **Dose History** screen (PRO tier), exportable as email. Delete sits in edit mode. Notifications are per-medication; pause exists as a "Postpone" action on the reminder. Visual rhythm is dense table-row vernacular — Dosecast prioritises information density over hero typography. The rolling-cadence affordance is the single most copied pattern in the category.

## 6. Shotsy + Glapp — GLP-1, titration-native

Both apps build the detail page around the titration arc. Shotsy's surface stacks **Medication Name + Dosage Strength**, then a **"Preview Estimated Levels"** chart that morphs as the dose changes, then **Shot History** filtered by medication ([Shotsy App Store](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/shotsy-glp-1-tracker/id6499510249); [Shotsy site](https://shotsyapp.com/)). Titration is first-class: an "advanced scheduling system [that] allows you to set more precise schedules for your GLP-1 medications, including separate schedules, countdowns, and reminders for multiple concurrent medication types" — each dose step is its own scheduled entity. Edit/delete is bulk: **Show all records** inside **Manage My Data**, supporting bulk selection — a structural concession to chronic-care users who accumulate weeks of identical-shape records. Glapp's detail surface highlights "live drug cycle and medication levels", a visualised **drug cycle after each shot**, and the ladder ("2.5 mg → 5 mg → 7.5 mg → higher doses") rendered as a stepper rather than free-text ([Glapp site](https://glapp.io/); [GLP1 Effect](https://glp1effect.com/p/are-glp-1-app-medication-charts-reliable)). Both treat the medication-level chart — not the intake list — as visual hero. The lesson for HealthLog: for any course-window or titration-shaped medication, the **dose ladder** is the headline; the intake list is supporting context.

## Synthesis

### A. Detail-page architecture verdict

Section order for `/medications/[id]/page.tsx` — argued from the evidence above, optimised for HealthLog's chronic-care + titration-aware reality:

1. **Header band** — drug name + current-dose + status pill (`Aktiv` / `Pausiert` / `Beendet`) + small icon. Single accent colour per medication (user-picked on creation, mirrors Round Health). Pure typographic hierarchy, no chrome.
2. **Today's dose card** — one tap to log `Genommen` / `Übersprungen` / `Verschoben`. Mirrors Apple Health's pinned-today affordance; iOS muscle memory.
3. **Cadence summary** — single readable sentence ("Täglich um 08:00 + 20:00", "Wöchentlich, mittwochs", "Alle 12 h nach letzter Einnahme") with a **Bearbeiten** affordance opening the wizard. Dosecast proves this single-line summary works; Apple Health proves opening the editor as a dedicated surface protects against accidental rewrites.
4. **Dose ladder / phase configuration** — only rendered when the medication carries a titration plan or course window. Stepper visualisation (Shotsy / Glapp pattern), highlighting the current step. Hidden cleanly when not relevant — avoids the "advanced" trap MyTherapy falls into.
5. **Intake history** — preview of the last 7–14 entries, **Alle Einnahmen anzeigen** affordance opening a dedicated history surface for weeks/months. See section B.
6. **Notifications** — per-medication switch + reminder-window controls. Apple Health and MyTherapy don't surface this per drug; that's the gap HealthLog closes.
7. **Settings** — pause/resume, API-token linkage, external-logger integration, advanced cadence overrides. See section C.
8. **Destructive zone** — `Pausieren` (reversible), `Beenden` (preserves history, no future reminders — Apple's archive equivalent), `Löschen` (purges history; destructive confirmation). See section D.

This sequence puts daily actions (log today, see recent history) at the top of the scroll and rare ones (delete, advanced settings) at the bottom. Apple's Archive / Delete split is the model — but the Apple Discussions "where is delete?" complaints point at discoverability, not the split itself. Keep the split, label the destructive zone explicitly so it is findable on first scroll.

### B. Intake history pattern

Preview-then-expand. The detail page shows the last 7–14 events as a grouped list — **Heute**, **Diese Woche**, **Älter** — each row carrying timestamp, dose-as-taken, and a quiet status glyph (`Genommen` / `Übersprungen` / `Verschoben`). Each row swipes-to-reveal `Bearbeiten` and `Löschen` (iOS-native gesture; matches Apple Health's swipe-archive and MyTherapy's tap-day-to-edit). An **Alle Einnahmen anzeigen** affordance opens a dedicated history surface with month-grouped grouping, search by date, and bulk delete (Shotsy's `Show all records` pattern, justified by the chronic-care reality of accumulating weeks of identical-shape records). Empty state: `Noch keine Einnahmen erfasst.` paired with a single affordance to log the first — typography, not illustration. The verbatim DE labels: `Einnahmen`, `Heute`, `Diese Woche`, `Älter`, `Bearbeiten`, `Löschen`, `Alle Einnahmen anzeigen`, `Genommen`, `Übersprungen`, `Verschoben`, `Noch keine Einnahmen erfasst.`

### C. Settings consolidation

Dedicated `<SettingsSection>` on the detail page, **not** a separate Sheet/Drawer. Three reasons. First, Apple Health's official guide keeps `Details` and `Options` inline on the detail page — no separate sheet ([Apple Support](https://support.apple.com/guide/iphone/track-your-medications-iph811670c81/ios)). Second, Nielsen Norman is explicit: "a bottom sheet is a transient UI element that is not intended to be a stable place for users to return to" ([NN/G bottom sheets](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/bottom-sheet/)). Per-medication settings — notifications, pause, API token, phase config — are exactly the kind of surface a chronic-care user does return to. Third, the v1.5.4 narrow-asymmetric report — pushing settings into a sheet repeats that mistake. Inline keeps the scroll continuous, lets every control breathe at the same width as the cadence + history sections. Round Health and Dosecast keep settings inline; MyTherapy and Medisafe lean on edit-mode-on-the-detail-page rather than a sheet. Category consensus is inline.

API tokens and external-logger integration are HealthLog-specific. Place them as a labelled subsection inside `<SettingsSection>` titled `Externe Integration` — one row per token, masked, copy + revoke affordances. Marc's "ganze API Roots" complaint is solved by giving this section heading the same typographic weight as `Erinnerungen`, so it stops feeling like an afterthought.

### D. Pause / delete destructive flow

Apple Health's three-tier split is the strongest in the category. MyTherapy collapses pause into "skip today" — the user-voice forum makes clear that's the wrong abstraction. Medisafe lacks pause. Three-tier model for HealthLog:

- **`Pausieren`** — reversible, suppresses future reminders, preserves history, shows a `Pausiert` pill in the header. Tap to resume. No confirmation dialog — pause is fully reversible. Closes MyTherapy's user-voice gap.
- **`Beenden`** — Apple's Archive equivalent. Stops future reminders, preserves history, removes from the active list, surfaces under a `Beendete Medikamente` filter. Single-step confirmation (`Wirklich beenden?`).
- **`Löschen`** — purges everything including intake history. Two-step confirmation with the medication name typed back as a guard, matching the Apple Discussions-forum pattern of users accidentally deleting and losing weeks of compliance data ([discussions.apple.com](https://discussions.apple.com/thread/254888869)).

No undo banner. The audit log is the system-of-record; an undo affordance would imply soft-delete semantics medications don't honour.

### E. Visual rhythm + dialog polish

The v1.5.4 narrow-asymmetric report has direct evidence. iOS 26 design-press confirms two shifts. **Left-aligned text replaces centre-aligned** in alerts, onboarding, and sheets — "left-aligned text is easier to read, especially for longer paragraphs" ([learnui.design](https://www.learnui.design/blog/ios-design-guidelines-templates.html)). **Concentric corner radii** — a nested element's radius subtracts the padding from its parent, "which makes it easy to get the perfect corner radius for things like a button within a card" ([same source](https://www.learnui.design/blog/ios-design-guidelines-templates.html)). Linear adds: "not every element of the interface should carry equal visual weight — navigation should recede while task-critical areas remain prominent" ([linear.app refresh](https://linear.app/now/behind-the-latest-design-refresh)).

Token direction for the design-direction doc:

- **Dialog width**: `min(560px, calc(100vw - 32px))` on desktop, full-bleed on mobile. The v1.5.4 dialog was tighter and read as cramped; 560 px lets the cadence-summary line breathe.
- **Section padding**: 24 px outer, 20 px inner — concentric.
- **Section gap**: 32 px between top-level sections so the scroll reads as a document, not a stack of cards.
- **Card corner radius**: 16 px outer, 12 px inner — concentric, matches iOS 26 default.
- **Font cascade**: 28 pt / 700 drug name, 17 pt / 600 section headers, 15 pt / 400 body, 13 pt / 500 labels + timestamps, SF Pro Text / Inter fallback. Left-aligned throughout.
- **Single accent colour per medication** — user picks on creation, drives only the status pill and today-card border. Surface stays neutral grey + foreground; no category-coloured sections. Round Health's restraint over Medisafe's colour wheel.

Nielsen Norman's iOS 26 critique is blunt: "anything placed on top of something else becomes harder to see" ([nngroup.com on Liquid Glass](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/liquid-glass/)). HealthLog stays on opaque surfaces; the dignity comes from typography and spacing, not material effects.

### F. Status-bar morph animation

Pin to **Material 3 Expressive's shape-morph pattern**, not iOS 26's Liquid Glass. Three reasons. M3E's looping shape-morph "captures attention through fluid motion, morphing between a series of shapes" and was designed for the kind of multi-step progress narrative a course-window or titration ladder represents ([9to5Google M3E loading](https://9to5google.com/2025/05/16/material-3-expressive-loading-indicator/); [supercharge.design](https://supercharge.design/blog/material-3-expressive)). Google's research backed it as "more usable" across all age groups. iOS 26's Liquid Glass, by contrast, is reserved for the navigation layer — Apple's own guidance: "Liquid Glass is best reserved for the navigation layer that floats above the content of your app" ([developer.apple.com](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/SwiftUI/Applying-Liquid-Glass-to-custom-views)). A progress indicator sits in the content layer; using Liquid Glass there misuses the material. Nielsen Norman's critique — "motion for motion's sake is not usability. It's distraction with a side of nausea" — is aimed at gratuitous motion, but explicitly endorses motion that communicates state-change. A shape-morph advancing on intake or phase carries genuine state-change semantics.

Concrete direction: a horizontal progress band at the top of the detail page (under the header) that fills as the course window or titration ladder advances. The shape morphs at each step boundary — circle at step 0, rounded square at midpoint, elongated capsule at completion — using a Framer Motion `spring` with damping 25–30 (responsive, not bouncy). Stroke in subtle accent, fill neutral. For one-shot medications without a course window the band collapses to a thin line with an "Aktiv seit DD.MM." caption — same component, dignified rest state. M3E's seven-shape sequence is the upper bound on visual ambition; HealthLog needs three shapes because the state space is discrete and small.
